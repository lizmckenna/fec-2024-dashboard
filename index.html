With TV/radio media buying firms excluded, the two parties’ digital & tech ecosystems are more competitive than they appear. Key Observations

WinRed Spending Concentrated in Trump Committees

Trump’s committees account for 99.4% of all WinRed spending ($309M of $311M). No other Republican candidate appears as a significant WinRed client in FEC disbursement data, suggesting the platform functions primarily as Trump campaign fundraising infrastructure.

DNC Committees Account for Most CRM Spending

DNC and party infrastructure committees account for the largest share of NGP VAN spending ($76M of $79M) and EveryAction ($23M of $29M). Individual Democratic campaigns like Harris and Biden use ActBlue for fundraising but rely on party-level infrastructure for CRM and voter data — a centralized model distinct from the Republican approach.

Biden-to-Harris Infrastructure Transfer

Harris’s $81M in tech spending across ActBlue ($76M), NGP VAN ($2.6M), and EveryAction ($1.7M) built on Biden’s existing vendor relationships. Biden’s committees show $52M, mostly through ActBlue ($47M). The mid-cycle transition represented a large-scale migration of campaign technology infrastructure between candidates.

Anedot Is the Down-Ballot R Platform

While WinRed spending is concentrated in Trump committees, Anedot serves 50+ Republican House and Senate campaigns: Cruz ($1.1M), Moreno ($0.9M), Lake ($0.7M), Ramaswamy ($0.6M), and dozens more. Anedot represents a more distributed pattern of Republican fundraising platform adoption.

Democratic Digital Ad Spending Exceeded Republican by ~3:1

Harris campaign spent $113M on Meta vs Trump’s $17M. On Google/YouTube: Harris $31.5M vs Trump $9.3M. The scale of this spending disparity, combined with the election outcome, has prompted debate among practitioners about the relationship between digital ad volume and electoral returns.

Lower Trump Campaign Digital Ad Spending

The Trump campaign reported lower digital ad spending compared to prior cycles. Reporting suggests the campaign relied more on organic social media reach, earned media coverage, and super PAC expenditures, though the relative contribution of each channel is difficult to measure from FEC data alone.

Why FEC numbers undercount platform spending: Most campaigns don’t pay Facebook or Google directly. They pay media buying firms (Targeted Victory, Bully Pulpit Interactive, etc.) who then place the ads. The platform-reported transparency data ($1.35B on Google+Meta) comes from a completely different data pipeline than FEC filings. Only $96M of the $1.35B in platform-reported spending appears as direct vendor payments in FEC data — the other 93% flows through intermediary firms.
